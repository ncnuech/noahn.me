About Noah Nuechterlein

I am a software development engineer at Epic Systems in Madison Wisconsin. I received my Bachelor's degree in Computer Science Engineering from the University of Michigan in 2016. At Epic, I will be working on user facing applications which are designed to help inpatient clinicians quickly and easily understand patient data so that they can do their jobs quicker and better.

In my undergraduate pursuit, I was involved in imbedded systems research with Lab11, working for Professor Prabal Dutta. The lab's focus was on the Internet of Things and pervasive computing. Specifically solving the last inch problem, one step beyond the last mile problem. We like to say that "The world would be a better place when everything is networked and everything is connected". Expounding on this, we want to live in a world where a chair can tell you who is sitting in it and your refrigerator can query it's content and leave you a message dictating that you need to pick up milk on the way home from work. The solutions to this, at least right now, is sensors, tiny low power sensors everywhere. Of course this level of coverage is a ways off, but in my free time I'm slowly trying to build myself bits and pieces of a smart house, letting data and automation make my life easier.

I'm looking for projects to do in my free time to keep me up to date with the ever changing world of tech. If you have an idea or an app that needs a another part time developer feel free to reach out to me. And if you want to bounce ideas of someone, I'm always interested in talking about tech and application design.

    Projects and Experience

    EECS Help Application

    As a final project for a UI Course, as part of a group of three, I built a mobile application to aid CS instructors in office hours. In an attempt to reduce friction in giving office hours we created a mobile queue of students who can be viewed, serviced and removed from the queue. This aims to solve the problem of whiteboard signups which both do not scale, and require the instructor to waste time in order to find who the next student on the queue is. It should serve as a mobile workstation for instructors conducting office hours.

    VLC Localization

    As part of Professor Dutta's Lab, I implemented a piece of software which found the relative location of a camera to any known LED lights in the image. The project had a number of steps which follow.
    1. Setup Led light tubes, which blink at specific, unique frequencies.
    2. Start Software side by leveraging openCV to create a bounding rectangle around each "blob" of light in the image(blobs tend to be formed from more than a single light source because of the proximity of the 4 tubes inside of a single box light).
    3. Finding Changes in frequencies through probing the rectangles with FFTs. Using multiple points where the frequency change was over a noise threshold I created a line of best fit between the points minimizing the error while keeping the slope of the rectangle.
    4. With all boxes seperated, the code finds the center of each box(the center of each led tube) and takes the frequency at that point. The frequency is then compared to known position and frequency pairs. This is the end of my section of the code. In the implementation, these matches are passed off to angle of arrival calculations which find location relative to each light located in the image. The process is accurate to the decimeter level. The code can be found on Lab11's Github.

    Bang!

    As part of a team, at Mhacks we designed and developed the Android application "Bang!". Bang was designed such that on a single button press, a random contact from the user's contact list was selected, and paired with a random pickup line from a sqlite database. This pickup line is then automatically sent to the selected contact using Android's SMS Manager. The code for this project can be found on Github.
